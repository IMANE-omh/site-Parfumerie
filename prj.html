Perfume has a long and rich history that dates back to ancient civilizations. It first emerged around 4000 BCE in Egypt, Mesopotamia, and India, where aromatic resins, oils, and herbs were used in religious rituals, medicine, and daily life. The Greeks and Romans later refined perfume-making, turning it into a symbol of luxury and beauty. During the Islamic Golden Age, scholars like Avicenna advanced distillation techniques, leading to the creation of rose water and influencing modern perfumery. Perfume spread throughout Europe during the Middle Ages and Renaissance, becoming especially popular in Italy and France, where it was used to mask unpleasant odors and as a sign of elegance. By the 17th and 18th centuries, France—particularly Grasse—had become the center of perfume production, and the invention of alcohol-based perfumes allowed for lighter, longer-lasting fragrances. The 19th and 20th centuries brought synthetic ingredients, enabling new scent creations and the rise of famous perfumes such as Guerlain’s Jicky and Chanel No. 5. Today, perfumery continues to evolve, emphasizing sustainability, natural ingredients, and gender-neutral or personalized fragrances that reflect modern individuality and artistry
